Which embedding model best searches social work literature?

A head-to-head benchmark of twelve open-weight embedding models — plus cross-encoder rerankers — for semantic retrieval over the SWRD's research abstracts, entirely on local hardware. This is the study behind the semantic search built into these databases. The full write-up is a manuscript in preparation; this page summarizes the design and the current state of results, with preliminary figures labeled as such.

01 Why this study exists

Semantic search without the subscription

Strong semantic retrieval has typically depended on paid, proprietary embedding APIs — a problem for cost, privacy, and reproducibility in research settings. Social work adds a further wrinkle: domain vocabulary ("kinship care" vs "relative caregivers") that generic search handles poorly. This study asks whether open-weight models that run on ordinary hardware can deliver research-grade semantic search over the discipline's own literature — and at what computational cost. It extends the local-first agenda in social work AI research from classification tasks to retrieval.

Design

Corpus & queries

All 68,043 SWRD records with abstracts (title + abstract as document text; sources include Web of Science, Scopus, DOAJ, Digital Commons). 300 natural-language queries generated by a local language model from 150 sampled seed papers — two queries each — mimicking how researchers actually phrase questions.

Judging & metrics

Candidate results are graded 0–3 for relevance by a separate local model (a different family than the query generator, to avoid circularity), with a 150-pair human-expert validation sample. Primary metric: nDCG@10 with bootstrap confidence intervals. A keyword-search (BM25) baseline anchors every comparison.

Two-stage retrieval

Beyond single-model search, the study tests a "careful second look": a cross-encoder reranker that re-reads the top candidates side-by-side with the query and reorders them — slower per query, potentially more precise.

Cost as a first-class outcome

Every model's indexing throughput and peak memory are measured on the same machine (Apple M5-class, 64 GB), because for a lab or library the practical question is a quality-versus-cost frontier, not a single leaderboard winner.

What holds up so far

All twelve embedding models beat the keyword baseline. EmbeddingGemma (300M) is the standout for quality-per-size — within a few points of models 25× larger — and pairing it with the BGE cross-encoder reranker currently tops the board. That combination of quality, size, and zero cost is why this project's databases are encoded with it. EmbeddingGemma — like every model in this study — ran locally through Ollama on the benchmark machine, the same setup the project's skill files walk readers through.

Notable negative results

Reranking is not automatically better: one heavily-promoted reranker (mxbai-rerank-large-v2) consistently degraded results on this corpus, and another was roughly neutral. And bigger isn't uniformly better among embedders either — several mid-size models trail smaller ones. Domain fit matters more than parameter count.

A comparison against commercial embedding APIs (e.g., OpenAI's) is deferred to the completed study.

The top quality models (Qwen3 4B/8B) index at ~8–9 documents per second and want 17–30 GB of memory; EmbeddingGemma indexes ~105/second in a fraction of that — over an order of magnitude cheaper for a few points of nDCG that a reranker recovers anyway. At 68k documents, no approximate-nearest-neighbor index was even needed: exact search suffices at this scale.
